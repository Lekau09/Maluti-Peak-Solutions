7 CV Design Tips That Will Actually Get You Noticed

By Maluti Peak Solutions May 2025 4 min read
📅 Published: May 2025 🕐 4 min read 📌 Career

Recruiters and hiring managers spend an average of 6–10 seconds scanning a CV before deciding whether to read it properly or move on. That's it. In those few seconds, your layout, structure, and visual clarity determine whether your qualifications even get a chance to speak for themselves.

These seven tips focus on the design and presentation of your CV — because the most impressive experience in the world won't get noticed if it's buried in a cluttered, hard-to-read document.

Tip 1: Use One Font, Two Weights

A common mistake is using multiple different fonts to make a CV look "designed." It usually has the opposite effect — it looks chaotic. Instead, pick one clean, professional font (Calibri, Garamond, Georgia, or a modern sans-serif like Inter or Lato) and use bold and regular weights to create hierarchy.

Your name and section headings can be bold and slightly larger. Everything else stays in regular weight at a consistent size. Simple, clean, readable.

Tip 2: Give Your CV Room to Breathe

White space is not wasted space — it's what makes a document readable. Cramming as much text as possible onto the page might seem like a way to show how much you've done, but it actually makes reviewers less likely to read anything at all.

Use consistent margins (at least 1.5–2cm on all sides), leave space between sections, and don't compress your line spacing below 1.15. Your CV should feel like it's easy to read — because it is.

Tip 3: Lead With Your Strongest Material

Most people list their experience in reverse chronological order, which is correct. But the section order itself matters just as much. For most applicants, the order should be:

  • Contact information (clear, at the top)
  • A brief professional summary or objective (2–3 sentences)
  • Relevant experience
  • Education
  • Skills
  • Additional sections (awards, languages, references)

If you're a student with limited work experience, move Education up above Experience. Lead with what's most relevant to the role you're applying for.

Tip 4: Be Specific About Achievements, Not Just Duties

This is a content tip, but it affects how your CV reads visually too. Instead of bullet points that describe your job responsibilities, write bullet points that describe what you actually achieved:

  • Weak: "Responsible for managing social media accounts"
  • Strong: "Grew Instagram following from 200 to 2,400 in 6 months through consistent content and engagement"

Specific, measurable achievements are more believable and more memorable than vague duty descriptions.

Tip 5: Keep It to One or Two Pages

For most students and early-career professionals, one page is the right length. For people with more than 5–7 years of relevant experience, two pages is acceptable. Beyond that, you're almost certainly including things that don't add value.

Ruthlessly cut anything that doesn't directly support your application for the specific role. A shorter, tighter CV is almost always more effective than a longer, padded one.

Tip 6: Use ATS-Friendly Formatting

Many employers — especially larger organisations — use Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) that scan CVs before a human ever sees them. These systems can struggle with complex formatting, tables, text boxes, headers/footers, and some fonts.

To ensure your CV actually gets read:

  • Avoid putting important information in tables or text boxes
  • Use standard section headings ("Experience", "Education", "Skills") — don't get too creative
  • Keep your contact information in the main body, not in the header or footer
  • Save and submit as a PDF to preserve formatting, unless the employer specifically asks for Word

Tip 7: Tailor Your CV for Each Application

This is the most important tip of all — and the one most people skip because it takes extra time. A generic CV sent to 50 employers performs worse than a tailored CV sent to 10. Here's the minimum you should adjust for each application:

  • Update your professional summary to reflect the specific role
  • Reorder your bullet points so the most relevant experience comes first within each role
  • Mirror language from the job description (if they say "project management," use those words — not "managing projects")

This doesn't have to take hours. A well-designed base CV with a few targeted edits is the most effective approach for most job seekers.

Final Thought

A great CV isn't about making things look fancy — it's about making things easy to read, clearly structured, and directly relevant to what the employer is looking for. Get those basics right and your qualifications have a real chance to shine.
